1.4 Splitting Training and Validation Sets¶

Although in this coursework we will not use T-fold cross-validation in the standard form, it is important to know how to split the data into training and validation sets. Write a function that randomly divides the data set into a ‘training’ set with 80% of the rows, and a ‘validation’ set with the remaining 20%. Make sure that the training and validation sets have similar values for the Success Percentage.

Here, to make the validation set with similar success percentages as a training set, I create 20 bins to split our data based on values of Success Percentage. Each bin represents a range of 5%. Then, I sample 20% of every bin, dropping them from the training set. This means that the entire range of Success Percentage values is represented and that the values for the validation set will be more or less representative of the full data set. Below, I printed the average Success Percentage values. They're about 0.3% off which is very close.

In [63]:
#assume split of 20%
def split(X,y):
    X = X.copy()
    X["bin"] = pd.cut(X[y], 20)
    bins = [x for _, x in X.groupby("bin")]
    
    validation_set = pd.DataFrame()
    for b in bins:
        validation_set = validation_set.append(b.sample(frac=0.2))
    
    validation_set.drop("bin", axis=1, inplace=True)
    X.drop("bin", axis=1, inplace=True)
    X.drop(validation_set.index, axis=0, inplace=True)
    
    return X, validation_set
    
training_set, validation_set = split(data, "Success Percentage")
training_success_p = training_set.sum(axis=0)["Success Percentage"]/len(training_set)

Now, we look at sample errors for the training sample. I used the Mean Squared Error (MSE) and the Mean Absolute Error (MAE). MSE is useful because, by squaring the distances, it penalizes a model a lot more for making predictions that differ greatly from the actual expected value. In our case, climbing Moint Rainier, we could afford to be a few percents off but what we really want to avoid is to be very far off as that would become dangerous. Hence, the use of MSE.

MAE is more intuitive, calculating the average absolute distance between the predicted values and the actual values.

R-Squared score is a statistical measure of how close the data are fitted to the regression line; it's calculated as the model's explained variation over the total variation. Low scores (close to 0) mean that the model doesn't explain the variability in the set tested and that the model doesn't fit too well whereas high scores (close to 1) imply the model is a good fit for the data.

Everything is wrapped in a function as we're going to use it again in 2.1.2.

In [65]:
def compute_errors(regressor, X_train, Y_train, X_val, Y_val):
    
    Y_train_pred = regressor.predict(X_train)
    training_error_mse = metrics.mean_squared_error(Y_train, Y_train_pred)
    training_error_mae = metrics.mean_absolute_error(Y_train, Y_train_pred)

    print("MSE for training set: ", training_error_mse)
    print("MAE for training set: ", training_error_mae)

    Y_val_pred = regressor.predict(X_val)
    validation_error_mse = metrics.mean_squared_error(Y_val, Y_val_pred)
    validation_error_mae = metrics.mean_absolute_error(Y_val, Y_val_pred)

    print("MSE for validation set: ", validation_error_mse)
    print("MAE for validation set: ", validation_error_mae)
    
    print("Difference between the in-sample MSE and out-of-sample MSE: ", abs(validation_error_mse - training_error_mse))
    print("Difference between the in-sample MAE and out-of-sample MAE: ", abs(validation_error_mae - training_error_mae))
    
    print("Model R Squared value: ", metrics.r2_score(Y_val, Y_val_pred))

The three standard metrics to use in classifiers are precision, recall, and F1. They allow to for a more detailed analysis than just accuracy which can give an uncomplete picture of the model.

Here, we'll deal with True Positives (TP), False Positives (FP), False Negatives (FN), and True Negatives (TN)

$Precision = \frac{TP}{TP+FP} = $ truly successful attempts given it was classified as successful

$Recall = \frac{TP}{TP+FN} = $ the ability to identify successful attempts

$F1 = 2*\frac{Precision * Recall}{Precision + Recall} = $ the harmonic mean of both precision and recall

F1 is a useful measure because it takes into account both measures and penalises extreme values (by taking the harmonic mean instead of a simple average). Now, to analyze our results.

First, we see that the two classes (0, and 1) are well balanced in the validation set, differing in size by 38 samples. None of our precision and recall scores are high. Our precision average to 60%. For the case success=1, 40% of the time our classifier tells us we could summit Mt. Rainier we couldn't. This is an important metric as climbing the mountain could be dangerous and pushing climbers to go out in dangerous conditions is to avoid at all costs. Our recall is low as well, averaging at 60% again. This means we're missing a lot of good climbing days. It's a shame but it's less important than the precision metric in our situation as this would not put anyone in danger.

At this point, the F1 tells us what we already know - we've got a middling model that works barely above half the time. Below, we predict Success chances for the test set.
